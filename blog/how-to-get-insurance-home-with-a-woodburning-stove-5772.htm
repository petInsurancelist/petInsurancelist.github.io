How to Get Insurance Home with a Wood-Burning Stove

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Wood-burning stoves have made a comeback in recent years, thanks to their rustic charm, energy efficiency, and off-grid appeal. However, if you own or plan to install one, securing home insurance can be tricky. Many insurers view wood stoves as fire hazards, leading to higher premiums or even policy denials. But don’t worry—this guide will walk you through everything you need to know to get insured without breaking the bank.

Why Insurers Are Wary of Wood-Burning Stoves

Before diving into solutions, it’s important to understand why insurance companies hesitate to cover homes with wood stoves.

Fire Risk Statistics

According to the National Fire Protection Association (NFPA), heating equipment, including wood stoves, is a leading cause of home fires. Improper installation, lack of maintenance, or careless use can turn a cozy fire into a disaster.

Environmental Concerns

With climate change dominating headlines, some insurers are wary of policies that support carbon-emitting heating methods. While wood stoves are renewable, they still contribute to particulate pollution, which can be a red flag for eco-conscious insurers.

Liability Issues

If a fire starts due to your wood stove and spreads to neighboring properties, your insurer could face massive claims. This makes them extra cautious when underwriting such policies.

Steps to Secure Insurance for a Home with a Wood-Burning Stove

1. Choose the Right Insurance Provider

Not all insurers treat wood stoves the same. Some specialize in older homes or rural properties and may be more lenient. Consider:
- Regional insurers familiar with wood stove usage
- Companies with green energy policies that recognize EPA-certified stoves
- Independent agents who can shop around for the best deal

2. Ensure Proper Installation and Certification

Insurance companies will want proof that your stove is safely installed. Key requirements include:
- EPA-Certified Stoves: Modern, efficient models meet stricter emissions and safety standards.
- Professional Installation: Hire a licensed technician to ensure compliance with local codes.
- Permits and Inspections: Many areas require permits for wood stove installations. Keep documentation handy.

3. Implement Safety Measures

Reducing risk can lower your premiums. Consider:
- Installing heat shields to protect walls and flooring
- Using a spark arrestor on the chimney
- Keeping flammable materials at least 3 feet away from the stove
- Regular chimney cleaning (at least once a year)

4. Disclose Everything Upfront

Hiding your wood stove from the insurer is a bad idea. If a fire occurs and they discover an undisclosed stove, they may deny your claim. Be transparent about:
- The stove’s make, model, and certification status
- Installation details (who did it, when, and whether it was permitted)
- Any additional safety features you’ve added

5. Consider a Separate Wood Stove Policy

If your primary insurer refuses coverage, look into specialty insurers or add-on policies that specifically cover wood-burning stoves. These may cost extra but provide peace of mind.

How Wood Stoves Affect Your Premiums

Factors That Increase Costs

  • Older, uncertified stoves (higher fire risk)
  • Lack of safety features (no heat shields, improper clearance)
  • Location (urban areas with strict emissions laws may face higher premiums)

Ways to Lower Your Rates

  • Upgrade to an EPA-certified stove
  • Install smoke and carbon monoxide detectors
  • Bundle policies (e.g., home and auto insurance with the same provider)

The Future of Wood Stoves and Insurance

As sustainable living gains traction, insurers may adjust their policies. Some trends to watch:
- Incentives for eco-friendly stoves (e.g., pellet stoves with lower emissions)
- Smart stove technology (monitors that alert you to unsafe conditions)
- Government rebates for upgrading to cleaner-burning models

By taking the right steps, you can enjoy the warmth of a wood-burning stove without insurance headaches. Stay informed, prioritize safety, and work with the right insurer to protect your home.
