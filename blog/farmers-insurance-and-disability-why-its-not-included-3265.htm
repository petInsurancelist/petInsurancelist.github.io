Farmers Insurance and Disability: Why It’s Not Included

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Insurance is a safety net, a promise that when life throws curveballs, you won’t be left stranded. But what happens when that safety net has gaps? One glaring omission in many standard insurance policies—including those from Farmers Insurance—is disability coverage. In a world where workplace injuries, chronic illnesses, and mental health challenges are on the rise, the absence of disability protection raises critical questions. Why doesn’t Farmers Insurance include disability coverage in its standard policies? And what does this mean for policyholders navigating an uncertain future?

The Insurance Landscape: What Farmers Offers

Farmers Insurance is a household name in the U.S., known for its auto, home, and life insurance products. Their policies are designed to cover everything from fender benders to natural disasters. But when it comes to disability insurance—a product that replaces lost income if you’re unable to work due to injury or illness—Farmers falls short.

Standard Policies vs. Add-Ons

Unlike health or life insurance, disability coverage isn’t typically bundled into Farmers’ core offerings. Instead, it’s often treated as a supplemental product, something you must seek out separately. This approach isn’t unique to Farmers; many insurers treat disability coverage as an optional extra rather than a necessity. But in an era where 1 in 4 Americans will experience a disability before retirement, this exclusion feels increasingly out of touch.

Why Disability Insurance Is Often Overlooked

Misconceptions About Risk

Many people assume disabilities only result from catastrophic accidents—think car crashes or workplace injuries. But the reality is far broader. Chronic conditions like diabetes, arthritis, or even long COVID can render someone unable to work for months or years. Mental health struggles, including severe depression or anxiety, also qualify as disabilities under many policies. Yet because these conditions aren’t as visible, they’re often underestimated.

Employer Coverage Gaps

Some policyholders skip disability insurance because they assume their employer’s benefits will cover them. While many companies offer short-term disability (STD) or long-term disability (LTD) plans, these policies often have limitations. For example:
- STD may only cover a portion of your salary for a few months.
- LTD might kick in after a long waiting period, leaving you financially vulnerable.
- Self-employed individuals are especially at risk, as they rarely have employer-backed options.

Farmers’ decision to exclude disability coverage shifts the burden onto consumers, forcing them to navigate a fragmented system.

The Financial Fallout of Going Uninsured

The High Cost of Disability

Without disability insurance, a single injury or illness can derail your finances. Consider these sobering stats:
- The average long-term disability claim lasts nearly three years.
- Medical bills are the leading cause of bankruptcy in the U.S.
- Only 40% of Americans have enough savings to cover a $1,000 emergency.

For Farmers customers relying solely on auto or home insurance, a disability could mean choosing between paying rent or medical expenses.

Social Safety Nets Aren’t Enough

Some assume government programs like Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) will fill the gap. But SSDI approval is notoriously slow and difficult to secure. In 2022, over 60% of initial SSDI applications were denied. Even if approved, benefits average just $1,358 per month—far below what most families need to survive.

Why Farmers Doesn’t Include Disability Coverage

Profitability vs. Necessity

Insurance companies operate on risk assessment. Disability insurance is inherently risky because payouts are frequent and long-term. Unlike auto claims (which are often one-time events), disability claims can span decades. For insurers, that’s a financial liability.

Regulatory and Market Factors

State regulations also play a role. While auto and home insurance are heavily mandated, disability insurance is less regulated, allowing companies like Farmers to sidestep it in standard packages. Additionally, the market for standalone disability policies is competitive, with specialized providers like Mutual of Omaha or Guardian often dominating the space.

What Policyholders Can Do

Exploring Supplemental Options

If you’re a Farmers customer, don’t wait for a crisis to strike. Consider these steps:
1. Ask About Riders: Some life insurance policies offer disability riders for added protection.
2. Shop Standalone Policies: Companies like Breeze or Policygenius specialize in disability coverage.
3. Maximize Employer Benefits: If your job offers LTD, understand the terms and supplement gaps with private insurance.

Advocacy and Awareness

The more consumers demand disability coverage, the more insurers will listen. By pressuring companies like Farmers to rethink their offerings, policyholders can drive change in the industry.

The Bottom Line

Disability isn’t a remote possibility—it’s a statistical likelihood. Yet Farmers Insurance and many others treat it as an afterthought. In a world where health and financial stability are increasingly precarious, that’s a risk too big to ignore.
