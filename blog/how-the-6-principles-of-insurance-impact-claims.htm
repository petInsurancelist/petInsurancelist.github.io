How the 6 Principles of Insurance Impact Claims

Home / Blog / Blog Details

The world feels increasingly unpredictable. From climate-fueled megafires and historic floods disrupting global supply chains, to cyberattacks crippling hospitals and businesses, to a pandemic that rewrote the rules of risk itself, the frequency and severity of losses are escalating. In this era of heightened uncertainty, the insurance contract becomes a critical lifeline. But what many policyholders don’t realize is that the entire process of filing and settling a claim—the moment of truth for any insurance policy—is governed by a set of ancient, unwavering legal doctrines. These are the six fundamental principles of insurance.

They are not just abstract legal concepts buried in the fine print; they are the very DNA of every claim. They determine what is covered, how much is paid, and whether a claim is paid at all. Understanding these principles is no longer a matter of academic interest but a practical necessity for navigating the complex claims arising from today’s most pressing global issues.

The Bedrock of Trust: Why These Principles Matter More Than Ever

In a digital age where new risks emerge faster than policies can be written, these principles provide a stable framework for adjudicating fairness for both the insurer and the insured. They prevent moral hazard, ensure equitable sharing of risk, and ultimately, keep the entire global insurance mechanism solvent. Let’s explore how each one directly impacts the claims process in the context of 21st-century challenges.

1. Principle of Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)

This is the cornerstone principle, mandating that both parties to the insurance contract must act with complete honesty and disclose all material facts.

How it Impacts Claims: This principle is activated at the application stage but is tested most severely during a claim. In an age of complex risks, an insurer’s investigation post-claim is deeper than ever. For instance, a company filing a hefty business interruption claim following a ransomware attack might find their entire claim denied if it’s discovered they failed to disclose a previous, smaller cyber incident when applying for the policy. That prior event was a material fact—one that would have influenced the insurer’s decision to offer coverage or set the premium.

With the rise of IoT devices and telematics (like black boxes in cars or sensors in factories), the definition of "disclosure" is expanding. An applicant’s data footprint itself can be a material fact. A claim for a stolen vehicle could be jeopardized if telematics data reveals a history of reckless driving that was not disclosed. In a world of big data, the duty of utmost good faith is a two-way street; insurers must also be transparent about how they use this data in claims assessment.

2. Principle of Insurable Interest

This principle states that the person claiming must stand to suffer a financial loss from the damage or loss of the insured item. You cannot insure something on which you have no financial stake.

How it Impacts Claims: This principle directly prevents insurance from becoming a speculative wager. Consider the massive global supply chain. A single shipping container on a vessel like the Ever Given can contain goods owned by a manufacturer, financed by a bank, and destined for a retailer. If the container is lost overboard, multiple parties might file a claim. The principle of insurable interest requires each entity to prove its specific financial stake in those exact goods. The bank must prove its secured interest, the retailer must prove title has passed to them, and the manufacturer must prove they still owned it. Without demonstrable insurable interest at the time of loss, the claim is invalid.

This is also crucial in life insurance and has modern implications. With the growth of the "gig economy," can a food delivery platform like DoorDash or Uber insure its thousands of independent contractors? Likely not, as the company lacks an insurable interest in the lives of its contractors—it does not suffer a direct financial loss upon their death. This principle draws a clear legal boundary around who can claim.

3. Principle of Indemnity

The principle of indemnity is designed to restore the insured to the same financial position they were in immediately before the loss—no more, no less. It prevents profiting from a loss.

How it Impacts Claims: This is the heart of most property and casualty claims. It’s why you receive the Actual Cash Value (depreciated value) of a stolen 5-year-old laptop, not a check for a brand-new model. However, modern disputes are far more complex. In climate-related claims, such as a house destroyed by a wildfire, does "indemnity" mean rebuilding the exact same home, or must it be rebuilt to new, stricter building codes? Typically, a standard policy will pay to rebuild the like-kind home, but code upgrade coverage can be added for an extra premium.

In business insurance, a lengthy interruption due to a cyberattack can lead to lost profits. The principle of indemnity aims to calculate these lost earnings accurately, but disputes often arise over the projection of future earnings. The insurer will indemnify for the loss, but they will not write a blank check for hypothetical, exaggerated future growth.

4. Principle of Contribution

This principle comes into play when two or more policies cover the same risk and the same interest. It ensures that no insured profits by claiming the full amount from multiple insurers.

How it Impacts Claims: In our interconnected world, overlap is common. A professional consultant might have a professional liability policy and also be covered under a client’s overarching policy for a project. If a negligence claim is filed, the principle of contribution dictates that the insurers will share the loss proportionately. The insured cannot "double-dip" and collect from both, as that would violate the principle of indemnity.

This is critically important in large-scale liability cases, such as those involving environmental pollution or product liability, where multiple companies (and their insurers) may be held responsible. Contribution agreements between insurers are essential to untangle these complex claims and ensure a fair apportionment of the settlement costs.

5. Principle of Subrogation

Subrogation is the right of the insurer, after paying a claim, to legally pursue a third party that caused the loss. This right is transferred from the insured to the insurer.

How it Impacts Claims: This principle is a direct enforcement of indemnity—it prevents the insured from being compensated twice (once by the insurer and once by suing the negligent party) and helps keep premiums lower by recovering funds. Its application is everywhere in modern claims.

  • Cybersecurity: If Company A’s network is breached because of a vulnerability in software provided by Company B, Company A’s cyber insurer, after paying the claim, may subrogate and sue Company B for damages.
  • Supply Chain: If a fire at a subcontractor’s factory halts your production, your business interruption insurer may pay you and then sue the subcontractor (or their insurer) for negligence.
  • Auto Insurance: This is the most common example. Your insurer fixes your car after a not-at-fault accident and then pursues the at-fault driver’s insurance company for reimbursement.

For the policyholder, the impact is significant. To preserve the insurer’s subrogation rights, you cannot admit fault or release a third party from liability after a loss. Doing so could jeopardize your entire claim.

6. Principle of Proximate Cause (Causa Proxima)

This principle seeks to identify the dominant, most direct cause of a loss when multiple events lead to the damage. Coverage depends on whether this proximate cause is a peril insured against.

How it Impacts Claims: This is perhaps the most contentious principle in today’s world, especially with climate change. Policies often exclude certain perils like flood or earthquake. The dispute often hinges on causation.

  • Scenario: A hurricane (often an excluded flood peril) hits a coast. Storm surge (flooding, excluded) destroys the foundation of a home, and then high winds (a covered peril) cause the structure to collapse. What was the proximate cause? The insurer may argue the flood was the dominant cause and deny the claim. The policyholder may argue the wind was the final, decisive blow. These legal battles are fought in thousands of claims after every major hurricane.

  • Pandemic: Was the proximate cause of a business closure the virus itself (often excluded as a pathogen), or was it the government-mandated lockdown orders (which might be covered under certain policy wordings)? Courts around the world grappled with this exact causation debate during COVID-19, with billions of dollars in claims hanging in the balance.

The principle of proximate cause forces a forensic analysis of the chain of events, making it the central battleground for complex, catastrophic claims.
